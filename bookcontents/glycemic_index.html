Glycemic Index

The Glycemic Index (GI) is a ranking system that classifies carbohydrate-containing foods based on how quickly they raise blood glucose levels. Foods are scored on a scale of 0 to 100, with pure glucose (which rapidly spikes blood sugar) set at a reference point of 100. Foods are typically classified into three categories:

  1. Low GI (0-55): Foods that are digested, absorbed, and metabolized slowly, leading to a gradual increase in blood glucose. Examples include lentils, nuts, and many fruits and vegetables.
  2. Medium GI (56-69): Foods that have a moderate effect on blood sugar. Examples include bananas, quick oats, and brown rice.
  3. High GI (70 and above): Foods that cause a rapid spike in blood glucose. Examples include white bread, potatoes, and sugary beverages.

Why GI Matters:

The GI helps people, especially those managing diabetes or aiming to maintain steady energy levels, choose foods that minimize blood sugar spikes. Low-GI foods can improve satiety, support weight management, and reduce the risk of insulin resistance.
